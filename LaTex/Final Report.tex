\section{Introduction}
\textit{Motivation} Beyond the pursuit of generating infinite wealth by predicting the future values of the stock market (or inadvertently collapsing any concept of having a financial market to begin with), simply being content with being able to predict the direction of the market (emphasis on the US stock market) of the following day allows for individuals to trade and/or invest more wisely. The motivation behind understanding this problem, and subsequently attempting to solve it, is given by the consequences it could bring to the financial industry. 

In addition, there is an interesting discussion on how efficient the market is. If the market is efficient, then it should be impossible to predict the direction of the market with any amount of data. If the market is not efficient, then it should be possible to predict the direction of the market with some amount of data. The real world data might indicate a stage between the two extremes, which is also interesting to analyze: morderate past information may contribute to the prediction of the market direction, but the predictive power of such data may diminish as more longer past values is added.

This paper will shed a light on whether or not the aforementioned data is adequate towards predicting the direction of the US stock market, and if not, gives rise to the question of whether or not it is possible to find any amount of data that will.     

\subsection{Research problem}

Our project aim at predicting the Direction of the S\&P500 Index. More specifically, we're interested in determining the direction of the market on the following day (on a day-to-day basis). Along with the prediction, we indirectly answer the question of how efficiency of the market is, and whether or not there are any patterns in the data that can be utilized to predict the market direction.

\subsection{Background context}

The S\&P 500 Index is a stock market index that measures the stock performance of 500 largest companies in terms of market capitalization listed on the stock exchanges in the United States. This index is one of the most commonly followed equity indices and is often used as a benchmark for the overall performance of the U.S. stock market. The index includes companies from various sectors, such as technology, healthcare, financial services, consumer discretionary, and more. The S\&P 500 Index is widely regarded as a leading indicator of the health of the U.S. economy and is closely watched by investors, analysts, and policymakers.

The computation of the index employ the float-adjusted market capitalization (FMC), which accounts for only the shares that can actually be traded in the market, in the weighting process. This means that the companies with larger market caps are weighted more heavily, making their positive/negative share price movements much more impactful on the broader index.

Predicting the direction of the stock market is a complex task that has been studied extensively in the field of finance and economics. The stock market is influenced by a wide range of factors, including economic indicators, company performance, geopolitical events, and investor sentiment. As a result, accurately predicting the direction of the stock market can be challenging, and there is no single method that can guarantee success. This project will explore various machine learning techniques to predict the direction of the S\&P 500 Index based on historical data. Part of the motivation for using historical data only is that the market already reflect reactions to economic and political environment, and part of the motivation is because the concurrent economic and political indexes affecting market are usually not available for the period of prediction. Futhermore, beside the interest of understanding the market, the key focus of this project is evaluating the performance of different machine learning models within the context of less than perfect information situation.

\subsection{Dataset description}

Data include price metrics and volumn traded of stocks that are part of the S\&P 500 index spanning January 1st, 2018, to December 31st, 2025. We removed rows based on whether or not a holiday was observed by all stocks (in other words, all values in one row for stocks were NA). The resulting preprocessed dataset should contain no NAs. Some further cleaning was performed to remove stocks that were listed on a date past January 1st.

The raw data loaded from Yahoo Finance contains open price, close price, high price, low price, and traded volume for each stock. The data is then engineered to create additional features that are commonly used in financial analysis and trading strategies. These features include lag values, percent change, volatility, max/min channel position, rolling volume weighted average prices, rolling z-scores, daily range, and day of week. 

Lag captures momentum, which will allow us to see if past data has any impact on future values. Except for the setting of the neural network methods to include each data points in the model, all other methods for prediction requires additional predictors of lag values to be created before the training process. Different lag periods are used and the results will be compared to see if there is any significant difference in the performance of the model. This feature is particularly important for the machine learning method that is different from tradditional economic time series analysis method, as it allows us to capture the relationship between past and future values in a more flexible way.

Regarding other created features, the percent change is a common way to measure the relative change in price over time, which can be more informative than absolute price changes. The volatility feature captures the variability of price movements, which can be an important factor in predicting future price movements. The max/min channel position can help identify potential support and resistance levels, which can be useful for predicting future price movements. The rolling volume weighted average price (VWAP) can provide insight into the average price at which a stock has traded over a certain period of time, which can be useful for predicting future price movements. The rolling z-score can help identify outliers in the data, which can be useful for predicting future price movements. The daily range can provide insight into the volatility of a stock, which can be useful for predicting future price movements. Finally, the day of week feature can capture any potential patterns in stock price movements that may occur on specific days of the week. 

% At the end of our analysis, features involving non-stationary metrics (EMA, OP, CP, HP, LP, VWAP) are not included in the final model as they do not contribute to the prediction of the market direction. The final model will only include features that are stationary and have a significant impact on the prediction of the market direction.


The raw data loaded contains 501 rows, and 2520 columns (roughly around 20 megabytes worth of data). The sizable number of columns is explained by each stock (503) and index (1) having their own price metrics for CP, OP, HP, LP and TV $([503+1] * 5)=2520$. The number of rows is explained by the exclusion of 19 holidays and 211 weekend days.

Overall, the data is characterized by a large number of features (columns) compared to the number of observations (rows), which can pose challenges for traditional statistical methods. Additionally, the data is time series in nature, which means that there may be temporal dependencies and patterns that need to be accounted for in the analysis. These characteristics of the data will influence the choice of methods and techniques used for prediction and analysis in this project.

\section{Methods}
As our data set is time series and has a large number of features compared to the number of data points, some methods prove to have more merits, namely random forest tree classification, support vector machine, and neural networks (CNN, simple RNN, LSTM). These methods, however, require a certain level of computational capacity, which is not very extensive giving our constraints of time and resources. At the same time, basic generalized linear model like logistic regression is still applicable with additional dimensional reduction technique (Ridge, LASSO, and PCA). We have not concluded which are 3 methods should be focus in this project. Instead, we provided all preliminary result and seek for further advices. 


Logistic Regression with dimensional reduction or feature selections is our simplest model to be implemented. This method would result in a predicted response that measures the probability of the following day being a positive day. As it requires that the number of predictors smaller than the number of data points, we have to apply some dimensional reduction techniques, namely Ridge and LASSO regression, along with Principal Component Analysis. The necessity to use these techniques is further emphasized by the fact that there are systematic market movement making data of different stocks highly correlated. Also, multiple features are secondary indicators constructed from the raw data, resulting in the same issue of multicollinearity. Though the loss of interpretability could discourage us from using PCA and Ridge regression, our primary focus is on the prediction of the market direction is for their use. In almost all cases, there was data scaling (via StandardScaler provided by scikit-learn) performed when doing dimensionality reduction.

 With the available logistic regression method provided by the library scikit-learn, LogisticRegressionCV and LogisticRegression, a penalty term was inherently already a part of initializing the model. However, there was still another motivation for us to reduce the amount of features (columns) before fitting logistic regression, since it would otherwise take an extended period of time to run (especially since convergence would be impossible with stocks or engineered features that experienced a great deal of multicollinearity); PCA, Ridge, and LASSO method are used in this feature selection/engineering. As the number of features is very large, we do not use traditional method of forward-stepwise/ backward-stepwise/ forward-stagewise/ best subset selection. 

The second method experimented by our group is Random Forest Tree Classifier. This method would directly forecast the direction and is not restricted by the requirement of $p<n$ like in the  generalized regression model. This allows us to fit the model with all features, and through feature-importance, deduce which features carry the biggest weight. Because of a large number of predictors, we do not start with a base tree classifier. Random Forest model has a merit in this case as it allow us build a tree with a cap of feature number. 

There was some initial exploration into utilizing a random forest regressor then converting it's predictions into a binary response by checking for a positive or negative prediction. However, due to time-constraints, the exploration was stopped and only the random forest classifier was continued with. In this case, though the random forest classifier did not enforce that the number of parameters be less than that of the number of observations, there is no indication in the code that it was explored as a viable model. Instead, other dimensionality reduction methods were applied standalone (no combination of such methods), including: PCA, Ridge regression, LASSO regression, and Step-wise Regression utilizing walk forward validation.

The third method employed is Support Vector Machine. Similar to Random Forest method, it does not require $p<n$. Further more, SVM is a straight forward solution to a binary classification problem with more features than data points as it use kernel to capture data point proximity. We started our SVM model with a linear kernel as large feature number but finally choose RBF kernel due to its better performance. 

Additional  method employed in our analysis is neural network with the focus on convolutional, simple recurrent, and LSTM ones (7 days window). As the neural network learning algorithm already use all data points in constructing each nodes, it is not necessary to use lag values as predictors in these models. Among CNN, simple RNN, and LSTM, the most natural option among them is the last one because its nature is very close to the market reaction in time series data. The other networks are included partly because of our concern about LSTM computing time and partly due to our curiosity of their application for time series. More specifically, 2D-CNN is possible for a time relationship between data points while a simple RNN model can reflect somehow the relationship of market movement at different times. Overall, the neural network method are feasible when we extended the time period for the data loaded. This method, however, is a black box and is not interpretable.   

Regarding to the selection of optimal parameters for the above mentioned methods,  beside standard k-fold cross-validation provided in the textbook that we are used for all models, walk-forward validation are employed in some feasible ones. For the models that is computationally intensive, the number of fold is set to be 5 while our previous milestone indicate 10-fold cross validation. The general selection of hyperparameter is based on the model with highest average cross validation accuracy while attain an acceptable level of standard deviation. Also, among several models of similar average cross validation accuracy, the more parsimonious one will be selected. Detailed of optimal hyperparameter for each model is in included in the appendix.

\subsubsection{Extended Feature Models}

This section will present the performance comparison of models that utilize the engineered features, including lag values, percent change, volatility, max/min channel position, rolling volume weighted average prices, rolling z-scores, daily range, and day of week. The models will include logistic regression with automatic regularization (LASSO and Ridge), random forest classifier, support vector machine with RBF kernel, and neural networks (CNN, simple RNN, LSTM). The results will be compared to the baseline models that only use raw features to evaluate the impact of feature engineering on predictive performance.

\input{model_comparison_table.tex}
\FloatBarrier

Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison} reveals that sequential models (RNN and LSTM) achieve the best overall performance with F1-scores of 0.6592 and 0.6571 respectively, benefiting from their ability to capture temporal dependencies in 7-day windows. The Simple RNN emerges as the recommended model for deployment, achieving the highest accuracy (0.5461) among all models while maintaining a good balance between precision (0.5535) and recall (0.8148). Among linear models, LASSO regularization (L1) demonstrates superior performance with the highest accuracy (0.5486) and the only ROC-AUC above 0.50 (0.5342), indicating actual discriminative ability beyond random guessing. Notably, the SVM with RBF kernel exhibits degenerate behavior, predicting all samples as ``Up'' (Recall=1.0) and achieving a ROC-AUC below random chance (0.4917), rendering it unsuitable for practical application despite a misleadingly high F1-score. The dimensionality reduction models (PCA, LASSO-reduced, Ridge-reduced) show moderate performance with accuracies ranging from 0.4632 to 0.5368, suggesting that while dimension reduction techniques help manage the curse of dimensionality with 41K features, they also risk losing important predictive signals. Overall, all models operate near 50-55\% accuracy, confirming that S\&P 500 direction prediction remains inherently challenging even with sophisticated machine learning approaches. Incorporating different metrics beyond accuracy, such as ROC-AUC and standard deviation of the cross validation accuracy, we propose the use of logistic regression with Ridge and LASSO regularization for the predictors included engineered features.
