\section{Introduction}

Beyond the pursuit of generating infinite wealth by predicting the future values of the stock market (or inadvertently collapsing any concept of having a financial market to begin with), simply being content with being able to predict the direction of the market (emphasis on the US stock market) of the following day allows for individuals to trade and/or invest more wisely. The motivation behind understanding this problem, and subsequently attempting to solve it, is given by the consequences it could bring to the financial industry. 

% In addition, there is an interesting discussion on how efficient the market is. If the market is efficient, then it should be impossible to predict the direction of the market with any amount of data. If the market is not efficient, then it should be possible to predict the direction of the market with some amount of data. The real world data might indicate a stage between the two extremes, which is also interesting to analyze: morderate past information may contribute to the forecasting of the market direction, but the predictive power of such data may diminish as more longer past values is added.

This paper will shed some light as to whether or not there is public data that is adequate towards forecasting the direction of the US stock market, and if not, gives rise to the question of whether or not it is possible to find any amount of data that will.     

\subsection{Research problem}

\quad \ Our project looks to forecast the direction of the S\&P500 Index of the following day (on a day-to-day basis). Consequently, we arrive at some potential conclusions regarding the question of how efficient the market is, and whether there are any meaningful patterns in the data that can be utilized to predict the market direction.

\subsection{Background context}

\quad \ The S\&P 500 Index is a stock market index that measures the stock performance of the top 500 companies in terms of market capitalization listed on the stock exchanges in the United States. This index is one of the most commonly followed equity indices and is often used as a benchmark for the overall performance of the U.S. stock market. The index includes companies from various sectors, including technology, healthcare, financial services, consumer discretionary, and more. The S\&P 500 Index is widely regarded as a leading indicator of the health of the U.S. economy and is closely watched by investors, analysts, and policymakers.

The computed valued of the index employs the float-adjusted market capitalization (FMC), which only takes into account the shares that are actually publicly traded on stock exchanges. This means that the companies with larger market caps are weighted more heavily, making the movements in their share price much more impactful on the broader index.

Forecasting the stock market is a complex task that has been studied extensively in the field of finance and economics. However, given by the fact that the stock market is influenced by a wide range of factors beyond company performance, including economic indicators, geopolitical events, and investor sentiment, accurately predicting the direction of the stock market is challenging. There is no known single method that can guarantee absolute success, but assuredly many that have come just a bit further than their predecessors. This project will explore various statistical learning techniques to attempt to forecast the direction of the S\&P 500 Index, and evaluate their performance based on historical data.

Part of the motivation for using historical data only is that the market already reflect reactions to economic and political environment, and part of the motivation is because the concurrent economic and political indexes affecting market are usually not available for the period of prediction. Futhermore, beside the interest of understanding the market, the key focus of this project is evaluating the performance of different statistical learning models within the context of less than perfect information situation.

\subsection{Dataset description}


\quad \ The raw data was retrieved via Yahoo Finance's API \verb|yfinance|, containing the open price, close price, high price, low price, and traded volume for each stock. The data utilized contain information on stocks that are and/or were listed on the S\&P 500 index during the time period spanning January 1st, 2018, to December 31st, 2025. The cleaning process pertained to evaluating the data for change in tickers (usually when one or more company merges with another company), invalid data (such as instances that indicated a stock was in a frozen state), holidays where the market was closed, and missing data due to being listed or delisted during the time frame. The resulting cleaned dataset should contain no NAs.

The features are then modified to create additional engineered features that are commonly seen used in quantitative technical analysis and trading strategies. These features include lag values, day-to-day percent change, volatility, max/min channel position, rolling volume weighted average prices, rolling z-scores, daily range, and the day of the week. 

A little emphasis on lag is provided as follows: lag captures momentum, which allows the model to see if past data has any impact on future values. Unlike recurrent neural networks, all other base models, which are being considered in this paper, require lag values to be created before the training process since time-series isn't built directly into their architecture. Different lag periods will be used and the results will be compared to see if there is any significant difference in model performance. This feature is particularly important for statistical learning models that aren't traditional economic time-series analysis models, allowing us to capture the relationship between past and future values in a more flexible way.

Regarding other created features, the day-to-day percent change is a common way to measure the relative change in price over time, which can be more informative than absolute price changes. The volatility feature captures the variability of price movements, which gives the model to potentially perceive highly volatile moments in the market. The max/min channel position helps identify potential levels of support and resistance, which can be used to inform the model on potential breakouts. The rolling z-score can help identify outliers in the data, which allows the model to perceive unnatural price movements (event detection). The daily range provides a glimpse into intraday volatility of a stock, which covers for what day-to-day volatility otherwise misses. Finally, the day of week feature can capture any potential patterns in stock price movements that may occur on specific days of the week. 

At the end of feature engineering, we remove most of the original data (OP, CP, HP, LP) and EMA (used to calculate Z-scores) since they are non-stationary, primarily due to fact that the architecture of Random Forest models will render most of the data obsolete and increase model complexity for no justifiable reason.

% At the end of our analysis, features involving non-stationary metrics (EMA, OP, CP, HP, LP, VWAP) are not included in the final model as they do not contribute to the prediction of the market direction. The final model will only include features that are stationary and have a significant impact on the prediction of the market direction.

The raw data contains 501 rows, and 2520 columns. The sizable number of columns is explained by each stock (501) and index (1) having their own price metrics for CP, OP, HP, LP and TV $([503+1] * 5)=2520$. The number of rows is explained by the exclusion of 19 holidays and 211 weekend days.

Overall, the data is characterized by a large number of features (columns) compared to the number of observations (rows), which can pose challenges for traditional statistical methods. Many engineered features are created based on raw features, leading to higher risk of multicollinearity problem. Additionally, the data is time series in nature, which means that there may be temporal dependencies and patterns that need to be accounted for in the analysis (such as what use was described for lag values). These characteristics of the data will influence the choice of methods and techniques used for forecasting and analysis in this project.

\section{Methods}

As our data set is time series and has a large number of features compared to the number of data points, some methods prove to have more merits, namely random forest tree classification, support vector machine, and neural networks (CNN, simple RNN, LSTM). These methods, however, require a certain level of computational capacity, which is not very extensive giving our constraints of time and resources. At the same time, basic generalized linear model like logistic regression is still applicable with additional dimensional reduction technique (Ridge, LASSO, and PCA). We have not concluded which are 3 methods should be focus in this project. Instead, we provided all preliminary result and seek for further advices. 

\quad \ Since the data is time-series in fashion and has a large number of features compared to the number of data points by default, some methods of classification prove to be more forgiving, particularly random forest classification and support vector machines (SVMs). However, in certain scenarios, like an unconventionally large feature space, these methods require a large amount of computational capacity, which given by the projects constraints on time and resources, were not always feasible. These issues are mostly alleviated when dimension of the feature space is sufficiently reduced. With this, methods like logistic regression are still applicable.

Furthermore, there exists quite a large amount of multicollinearity within the feature space, given straightforwardly by the fact that there are more features than observations, which needs to be addressed. This along with the issue of high dimensionality of the feature space can be addressed by certain dimensionality reduction and regularization methods include stepwise, LASSO and ridge regression, along with principal component analysis (PCA), collinearity reduction on stocks and features, grouping by sector averages and K-Means clustering. Each method provides different, or in some cases no, insight or interpretability of model results. Lastly, the data was scaled prior to fitting and subsequently predicting via StandardScaler implemented through a Pipeline (both provided through \verb|scikit-learn|).

It is good to note, before getting into the models that were utilized, that during implementation, different combinations of dimensionality reduction within the preprocessing of the data (such as the aforementioned grouping by sectors, or collinearity reduction) was applied to reduce computation time. Furthermore, in most cases, many different methods of dimensionality reduction and regularization were used to generate models differing in just that aspect. Lastly, in most cases (besides for when LogisticRegressionCV was used), \verb|GridSearchCV| was utilized to search for the optimal hyperparameters specific in dimensional reduction to each model type.

The first method to be employed is logistic regression. This model was available for use through \verb|scikit-learn| and had two variants: \verb|LogisticRegression| and \verb|LogisticRegressionCV|. In most cases, \verb|LogisticRegressionCV| was used to generate optimal $C$-values, and with a built in penalty flag for both ridge (L2) and LASSO (L1) (also including elasticnet), no external function was needed to reduce dimensionality prior to fitting the pipeline. However, in the case of the utilization of PCA, the ridge (L2) penalty was utilized internally after PCA was applied. In addition, the most basic logistic regression with PCA components and no regularization was also implemented for comparison.

Though the loss of interpretability could discourage us from using PCA and Ridge regression, our primary focus is on the prediction of the market direction is for their use. In almost all cases, there was data scaling (via StandardScaler provided by scikit-learn) performed when doing dimensionality reduction.

The second method that was utilized was the random forest classifier. This method is not restricted by the requirement of $p<n$ unlike logistic regression. This allowed for the model to be fit with all features. Furthermore, through feature-importance, we could deduce which features carry a meaningful weight. Due to the large number of predictors, we do not start with a decision tree classifier. Random Forest model has a merit in this case as it allow us build a tree with a cap of feature number. 

There was some initial exploration into utilizing a random forest regressor then converting it's predictions into a binary response by checking for a positive or negative prediction. However, due to time-constraints, the exploration was stopped and only the random forest classifier was continued with. Though the random forest classifier did not enforce that the number of parameters be less than that of the number of observations, 

The third method employed is Support Vector Machine. Similar to Random Forest method, it does not require $p<n$. Further more, SVM is a straight forward solution to a binary classification problem with more features than data points as it use kernel to capture data point proximity. We started our SVM model with a linear kernel as large feature number but finally choose RBF kernel due to its better performance. 

Additional  method employed in our analysis is neural network with the focus on convolutional, simple recurrent, and LSTM ones (7 days window). As the neural network learning algorithm already use all data points in constructing each nodes, it is not necessary to use lag values as predictors in these models. Among CNN, simple RNN, and LSTM, the most natural option among them is the last one because its nature is very close to the market reaction in time series data. The other networks are included partly because of our concern about LSTM computing time and partly due to our curiosity of their application for time series. More specifically, 2D-CNN is possible for a time relationship between data points while a simple RNN model can reflect somehow the relationship of market movement at different times. Overall, the neural network method are feasible when we extended the time period for the data loaded. This method, however, is a black box and is not interpretable.   

Regarding to the selection of optimal parameters for the above mentioned methods,  beside standard k-fold cross-validation provided in the textbook that we are used for all models, walk-forward validation are employed in some feasible ones. This method utilizes a shifting window of training (\verb|window_size|) and test splits (\verb|horizon|) that look to simulate how an investor and/or trader may view the market before making a decision. Given by the nature of past data becoming holding less predictive power over future events (which can be seen as a diminishing correlation between two days as they spread further apart), a more recent \verb|window_size| and an appropriately short \verb|horizon| would be preferable to stay consistent and in-alignment with the then present market regime. % For some neural network models that is computationally intensive, the number of fold is set to be 5 instead of 10. 

The general selection of hyperparameter is mainly based on the model with highest average cross validation accuracy. In addition, other metrics like ROC-AUC, F1-score, precision-recall, and ROC-AUC are also considered when they are available to specific models. Among several models of similar average cross validation accuracy, the more parsimonious one will be selected. Detailed of optimal hyperparameter for each model is in included in the appendix.

The models will include logistic regression with automatic regularization (LASSO and Ridge), random forest classifier, support vector machine with RBF kernel, and neural networks (CNN, simple RNN, LSTM). \input{model_comparison_table.tex}
\FloatBarrier

Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison} reveals that sequential models (RNN and LSTM) achieve the best overall performance with F1-scores of 0.6592 and 0.6571 respectively, benefiting from their ability to capture temporal dependencies in 7-day windows. The Simple RNN emerges as the recommended model for deployment, achieving the highest accuracy (0.5461) among all models while maintaining a good balance between precision (0.5535) and recall (0.8148). Among linear models, LASSO regularization (L1) demonstrates superior performance with the highest accuracy (0.5486) and the only ROC-AUC above 0.50 (0.5342), indicating actual discriminative ability beyond random guessing. Notably, the SVM with RBF kernel exhibits degenerate behavior, predicting all samples as ``Up'' (Recall=1.0) and achieving a ROC-AUC below random chance (0.4917), rendering it unsuitable for practical application despite a misleadingly high F1-score. The dimensionality reduction models (PCA, LASSO-reduced, Ridge-reduced) show moderate performance with accuracies ranging from 0.4632 to 0.5368, suggesting that while dimension reduction techniques help manage the curse of dimensionality with 41K features, they also risk losing important predictive signals. Overall, all models operate near 50-55\% accuracy, confirming that S\&P 500 direction prediction remains inherently challenging even with sophisticated machine learning approaches. Incorporating different metrics beyond accuracy, such as ROC-AUC and standard deviation of the cross validation accuracy, we propose the use of logistic regression with Ridge and LASSO regularization for the predictors included engineered features.

\subsection{Overfitting/underfitting analysis}


\quad \ A general note on overfitting and other issues with select models. Given by the fact that the raw data inherently has more features than observations, overfitting was a big issue, especially in earlier implementations of the model. Overfitting results in a train misclassification rate of zero perpetually and no amount of generalization was possible towards validation sets and test splits. Furthermore, partly because of this overfitting, when certain models (most notably logistic regression) would search for an optimal hyperparameter, since the training misclassification rate would be impeccable, if the lower bound of the $C$-values were small enough, the model would degenerate to always guessing 1 or 0 depending on the imbalance in the splits. 

This issue was mostly alleviated by introducing further dimensionality reduction methods (most notably grouping by sector and collinearity reduction), which reduced the dimensionality of the feature space enough for there to be any sense of meaningful training on the training set.

\subsection{Real-world implications}
 The accuracy of prediction in the best model is around 54\% suggests that there may be some predictive signal in the data. However, it is not very strong to consistently outperform random guessing. This has implications for traders and investors who may be looking for reliable methods to forecast market direction, indicating that relying solely on historical daily price and volume data may not be sufficient for making informed trading decisions. In contrast, the hourly models show better performance, which suggests that there may be more short-term dependencies in the market that can be captured with more granular data. This could have implications for high-frequency trading strategies and intraday trading.

\subsection{Limitations}

  This study has several limitations. In terms of data, we only used historical price and volume information and did not include sentiment analysis, macroeconomic indicators, or alternative data sources. In terms of modeling, we did not explore mixed-model approaches or more complex deep learning architectures. In terms of evaluation, we mainly relied on accuracy, F1-score, and ROC-AUC under walk-forward validation and cross-validation, while additional selection rules (such as the 1SE rule based on k-fold cross validation) could be considered in future work. Finally, both time and computational resource constraints limited the extent of hyperparameter tuning, model experimentation, and large-scale cross-validation.

\subsection{Possible methodological extensions}

Future methodological extensions include more extensive hyperparameter tuning and broader model experimentation, along with a more comprehensive evaluation framework that incorporates additional metrics and validation techniques. The analysis could also be strengthened by including political and economic indicators, sentiment analysis, and other alternative data sources to improve predictive power. In addition, future work should explore more sophisticated modeling approaches, including deeper neural network architectures and hybrid strategies that combine machine learning with classical economic time-series methods such as ARIMA and GARCH.
