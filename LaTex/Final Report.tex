\section{Introduction}

\quad Predicting the stock market is challenging yet crucial for investors, traders, and researchers \cite{LIN_2024}. Rather than aiming to predict exact future stock prices, this study focuses on the more practical goal of forecasting the next-day direction of the U.S. stock market using historical data on prices and trading volume. The motivation for studying this problem lies in its potential implications for the financial industry as well as economic discussion. 

The predictive performance of our models may inform the investors and policy makers about potential market reaction to previous trends. In addition, it also provide evidence on supporting or contradicting the Efficient Market Hypothesis. If our models achieve meaningful out-of-sample predictive accuracy using only such historical data, this would suggest that these variables contain some predictive content and would therefore be inconsistent with weak-form market efficiency. Conversely, if the models perform poorly, the results would be more consistent with weak-form market efficiency.

\subsection{Research problem}

\quad \ Our project looks to forecast the direction of the S\&P500 Index of the following day (on a day-to-day basis). Consequently, we arrive at some potential conclusions regarding the question of how efficient the market is, and whether there are any meaningful patterns in the data that can be utilized to predict the market direction.

\subsection{Background context}

\quad According to \citeauthor{wolfers_2004}, the Efficient Market Hypothesis states that market prices fully reflect available information \endnote{The Efficient Market Hypothesis states that market prices fully reflect available information. The form of the hypothesis depends on the type of information incorporated into prices.In its strong form, prices reflect all information; in its semi-strong form, prices reflect all publicly available information; and in its weak form, prices reflect information contained in past prices and trading data.}. This implies that excess returns should not be systematically predictable from the relevant information set. Under the weak form of the Efficient Market Hypothesis\endnote{See the preceding endnote.}, historical price and trading-volume data should therefore not contain exploitable patterns for predicting future returns. If our models achieve meaningful out-of-sample predictive accuracy using only such historical data, this would suggest that these variables contain some predictive content and would therefore be inconsistent with weak-form market efficiency. Conversely, if the models perform poorly, the results would be more consistent with weak-form market efficiency.

The S\&P 500 Index is a stock market index that measures the stock performance of the top 500 companies in terms of market capitalization listed on the stock exchanges in the United States \cite{spglobal_2024}. This index is one of the most commonly followed equity indices and is often used as a benchmark for the overall performance of the U.S. stock market. The index includes companies from various sectors, including technology, healthcare, financial services, consumer discretionary, and more. The S\&P 500 Index is widely regarded as a leading indicator of the health of the U.S. economy and is closely watched by investors, analysts, and policymakers \cite{investopedia_2024}.

The computed valued of the index employs the float-adjusted market capitalization (FMC), which only takes into account the shares that are actually publicly traded on stock exchanges. This means that the companies with larger market caps are weighted more heavily, making the movements in their share price much more impactful on the broader index.

Stock market forecasting is a complex task that has been studied extensively in the field of finance and economics. However, because the stock market is influenced by a wide range of factors beyond company performance, including economic indicators, geopolitical events, and investor sentiment, accurately predicting the direction of the stock market is challenging. No single method is known to remain uniformly superior over time; rather, many newer approaches have offered modest improvements over earlier methods that were once widely used \cite{KUMBURE_2022}. This project will explore various recent statistical learning techniques to attempt to forecast the direction of the S\&P 500 Index, and evaluate their performance based on historical data.

One motivation for using only historical data is that market prices may already incorporate responses to broader economic and political conditions. Moreover, the contemporaneous economic and political indicators that influence the market are often not available for the forecast period. Futhermore, the use of only historical data allows us to evaluate the predictive content of this information set, which is relevant for assessing the weak-form efficiency of the market as mentioned above.

\subsection{Dataset description}


\quad \ The raw data was retrieved via Yahoo Finance's API \verb|yfinance|, containing the open price (OP), close price (CP), high price (HP), low price (LP), and traded volume (TV) for each stock. They will be mentioned as OHLCV features. The data utilized contain information on stocks that are and/or were listed on the S\&P 500 index during the time period spanning January 1st, 2018, to December 31st, 2025, including both end. The cleaning process pertained to evaluating the data for change in tickers\endnote{this usually happens when one or more company merges with another company}, invalid data \endnote{for example, when a stock was in a frozen state}, holidays where the market was closed, and missing data due to being listed or delisted during the time frame. All the observations with missing information of any of the 5 price metrics or trading volume were removed.

The raw data contains 2009 trading days and 2520 features. The sizable number of columns is explained by each stock (503 tickers)\endnote{This differ from 500 tickers though the name of the index is S\&P 500. This is because some company ticker is not available for the whole period while some other company has more than one stock tickers listed in the market} and index (1) having their own price metrics for CP, OP, HP, LP and TV $([503+1] * 5)=2520$. The number of rows is explained by the exclusion of 19 holidays and 211 weekend days. 

The raw features are then modified to create target variables and additional features that are commonly seen used in quantitative technical analysis and trading strategies. Target variable is the binary value if the S \&P 500 goes up in the next day. Meanwhile, beside the day of the week (DOW - dummies), engineered features include lag values, day-to-day percent change, volatility, max/min channel position, rolling volume weighted average prices, rolling z-scores, daily range. Details of formula for each variable generation is provided in the Subsection \ref{subsec:feature_engineering} of the Appendix. 

Lag feature is particularly important for statistical learning models that aren't traditional economic time-series analysis models, allowing to capture the relationship between past and future values in a more flexible way. Currently 7 lag days are and the results will be compared to see if there is any significant difference. It should be noted that beside the reason that the engineered features are included by usual financial analysis, they are used in this project because of their potentials in capturing some level of non-linear relationship between raw data and target variabels. This is critically important in the case of employing generalized linear models in later data analysis. Finally, the day of week dunmies are added to reflect any potential cyclical patterns in stock movements. 

After completing feature engineering, most of the raw data (OP, CP, HP, LP) are removed from the engineered feature data to reduce data redundancy and limit their non-stationary problem in data analysis. The engineered data finally include 1981 data points with 13844 features.

\section{Methods}
The selection of methods in this project is highly depend specific characteristics of the data set, namely high-dimensional predictors, potentially high multicollinearity, and time-series structure, and unbalanced class in the binary classification problem.

First, high-dimensional features in the data set can cause the infeasibility of application for some methods while cost computational time for the others. Therefore, this characteristic need to be addressed well before further step of data analysis. Two methods are employed, namely Principaliple Component Analysis (PCA)\endnote{PCA and wavelet transform were the most popular methods used in literature, according to \citeauthor{KUMBURE_2022}} and LASSO regularization. Though the loss of interpretability could discourage us from using PCA, our primary focus is on the prediction of the market direction, not explanation of features' impact. 
The PCA method extract the M components that are highest in explaning the variance of the features as follows:
\[
z_{im} = \sum_{j=1}^p x_{ij} \phi_{jm}, \qquad m=1,\dots,M
\]
where $\phi_m$ is the largest $m$th loading direction.( Ch. 14, Section 14.5.1, \cite{ESL})
The LASSO regilarization perform optimization with the objective function include not only loss function but also the penalty term for absolute norm of the model coefficients as follows: 
    \[
    \hat{\beta}^{\mathrm{lasso}}
    = \arg\min_{\beta}\left\{
    \sum_{i=1}^n (y_i-\beta_0-x_i^\top \beta)^2
    + \lambda \sum_{j=1}^p |\beta_j|
    \right\}
    \]

Second, beside the high correlation of stocks' movement in raw feature set, the secondary indicators constructed from the raw data pause even a higher risk of multicollinearity problem. This can result in unstable result for model prediction and need to be addressed well. In this aspect, Ridge regularization will be used beside PCA and LASSO. Ridge regularization use the penalty for the second norm of the model parameters in the objective function for optimization is as follows: 
    \[
    \hat{\beta}^{\mathrm{ridge}}
    = \arg\min_{\beta}\left\{
    \sum_{i=1}^n (y_i-\beta_0-x_i^\top \beta)^2
    + \lambda \sum_{j=1}^p \beta_j^2
    \right\}
    \]( Chapter 3, Section 3.4.1, \cite{ESL})
    
Third, regarding time-series structure of the data set, two approaches are used in this project. One of them is create lagged predictors to preserve temporal ordering for the data analysis methods that are suitable with binary classification problem but not developed with inherent time-series natures. The other is to directly select binary classification methods with inherent time-series nature. The next paragraphs will discuss the selections of methods used for classification problem in more details, featuring Logistic Regression (LR), Random Forest (RF), Support Vector Machine (SVM), and Neural Network (NN).

Logistic Regression with dimensional reduction or feature selections throigh PCA and LASSO is our simplest model to be implemented because it fits with binary classification problem and it is within genralized linear models, the most classic approach. Logistic regression would result in a predicted response that measures the probability of the following day being a positive day as
    \[
    \Pr(G=1\mid X=x)
    =
    \frac{\exp(\beta_0+x^\top\beta)}
    {1+\exp(\beta_0+x^\top\beta)}
    \] (This is binary version for equation (4.17) in Chapter 4, Section 4.4, \cite{ESL})
It should be noted that logistic regression has its merits in the simplicity of being applied together with Ridge and LASSO regularization. The parameters need to be tune in this family of models include the number of PCA components and inverse regularization term (parameter C rather than $\lambda$ in Ridge and LASSO). As the number of features is very large, we do not use additioinal traditional methods of forward-stepwise/ backward-stepwise/ forward-stagewise/ best subset in feature selection for generalized linear models. 

The second method that is suitable to the binary classification problem as well as high-dimensional data in this project is Random Forest Classifiier (RF). This method directly forecast the direction and its computation is not restricted by the requirement of less features than data points like in the generalized regression model. RF perform feature-importance selection and deduce which features carry the biggest weight. It ensembles classification trees with the use of bootstrap sampling and use the majority vote for the final prediction (Chapter 15, \cite{ESL}). Parameters need to be tune in this method are maximum number of features in splitting, the depth of the tree, and number of trees used for aggregation. Though dimensional reduction technique is not required to apply this method, additional RF with dimensional reduction and regularization are still explored to reduce the instability of the model coefficients and performance.


The next method used for binary classification with high-dimensional features is support vector machine. Similar to Random Forest method, it does not require p < n. Further more, SVM is a straight forward solution to a binary classication problem with more features than data points as it use kernel to capture data point proximity in constructing decision boundary. Additionally, this method can work for both linear and non-linear separable data depending on the kenel used. In this analysis, I employed 3 different kernels, namely linear, polynomial and Radial Basis Function (RBF) one. The objective function for optimization in this method is as follows:
    \[
    \min_{\beta,\beta_0,\xi}\ 
    \frac{1}{2}\|\beta\|^2 + C\sum_{i=1}^N \xi_i
    \]
    \[
    \text{subject to } \quad
    y_i(x_i^\top\beta+\beta_0)\ge 1-\xi_i,\qquad
    \xi_i\ge 0,\quad i=1,\dots,N
    \] (Chapter 12, Section 12.2, \cite{ESL})

Additional method employed in our analysis is neural networks, including convolutional neural network (CNN with 2D design), simple recurrent neural networks (RNN), and long short term memory network (LSTM) with 7 days window. As the neural network learning algorithm already use all data points in constructing each nodes, it is not necessary to use lag values as predictors in these models. Among CNN, simple RNN, and LSTM, the most natural option among them is the last one because its nature is very close to time-series data. This is also the most applied method in recent year in stock market prediction \cite{KUMBURE_2022}. The inclusion of CNN and RNN in this project is due to initial concern of LSTM computing time and partly due to our curiosity of their application for time series. The parameters for tunning in neural network should be the number of hidden layers, number of nodes in those layers as well as epoch for training process. Within the scope of this project, only epoch is tunned while the structure is simplified for shor-time computation. It should be noted that the neural network method is a blackbox and is not interpretable. 

Regarding to the parameter tunning for the above mentioned methods, time series split and walk-forward cross validation are employed. This is to make sure there is no leakage of future information into the training and parameter tunning process which is likely caused by simple k-fold cross validation. To reduce the complexity of the models, as well as noise it may cause in prediction, parsimonious model is selected when similar performance are observed through 1SE rule (Chapter 7, \cite{ESL}). The metric used for parameter tunning process is balanced accuracy rather than simple validation accuracy to compensate for the bias of the training data (more up days).

To finalize which model should be employ in forcasting after the parameter tuning process, other metrics on the hold-out test set are used, including plain accuracy, area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (ROC-AUC), specificity, and sensitivity. While the test accuracy is a clear evaluation metric, ROC-AUC helps to access the performance of classifier at different threshold of decision making. Specificity and sensitivity evaluate the accuracy in smaller sub set of each binary class. In addition, the Matthews correlation coefficient (MCC) is also employed as a supporting metric. This coefficient evaluate overall performance of the classifier over all 4 cells of the confusion matrix \cite{chicco_2020}. For all of these metric, the higher values indicates a better classifier. Furthermore, diagnostic plots with validation balanced accuracy as well as its standard deviation are also employed in as supporting evidence for making the final decisions.

\section{Discussion}
This section summarize the implication of findings from the best model selected as well as its performance provided in previous section. 

\subsection{Interpretation of findings}
The result that 1 standard deviation of cross validation accuracy away from test accuracy of the best model does not include the randomguess  implies that the model is predictive and the historical data has some signals about the market direction. Even this signal is not strong as the testv accuracy is just around 55\%, this result still contradict the weak form of EMH. \endnote{Additional result for hourly data is included in the Appendix to investigate if there is any difference in historical signal in daily data and hourly data. The result shows a more predictive strength of the hourly data in even with logistic regression method - the poor performer in the leader board.}
\subsection{Real-world implications}
 As weak EMH is not supported based on some predictive strength observed in the best performed model, the historical data should remain as one of the reliable data source for forcasting the stock market. In addition, the specific model with accuracy around 55\% and cross validation accuracy standard deviation around 3\% constructed in this report can be utilized in forcasting market direction and the result can be used to design certain trade strategy with appropriate risk management. Furthermore, the accuracy of just 55\% from the historical data implies that the market forcast problem should utilize a more extensive data set more than just historical price and trading volume. 
\subsection{Limitations}
  This study has several limitations. In terms of data, only 8 years of daily data are used with servere bias toward upward direction of the market. In terms of modeling, only limited list of values are used in grid search for parameter tunning. Also, the deep learning architectures of the neural network models are very simple. In terms of evaluation, though variation of threshold in decision making in each classifier is accounted for by ROC-AUC metric, the actual accuracy, specificity, and sensitivity are not computed across different threshold while the plot for predicted probability by class clearly shows some potentially better threshold for the best model. These limitation can be relieved with more time and better computational resource.

\subsection{Possible methodological extensions}
Future methodological extensions include more extensive hyperparameter tuning, various choice of model decision threshold, and broader model experimentation, along with a more comprehensive evaluation framework. More specifically, for parameter tunning, a larger list for grid search in should be used. For decision threshold, potentially better one can be constructed by average median/ mean of actual class predicted probability. For cross validation selection rule, a cSE rule can be employed in the place of 1SE rule with various choice of c. For broader models, more complicated neural network structure can be used. And finalluy, for evaluation framewok, additional metric, namely Mathew Correlation Coeficient should be included. In addition, future work should explore potentials of hybrid strategies that combine machine learning with classical economic time-series methods such as ARIMA and GARCH.

\subsection{Details of Enginearing Features Before Dimensionality Reduction}
\label{subsec:feature_engineering}
Engineered predictors and targets are generated as follows:
\paragraph{(1) Percent-change base variables}
For each stock and day \(t\), percent-change features are computed from raw prices:
\[
\text{Close PC}_t=\frac{\text{Close}_t-\text{Close}_{t-1}}{\text{Close}_{t-1}},\quad
\text{Open PC}_t=\frac{\text{Open}_t-\text{Open}_{t-1}}{\text{Open}_{t-1}},
\]
and similarly for High/Low.

\paragraph{(2) Target construction}
The regression target is the next-day S\&P 500 open-to-close return:
\[
y^{(\mathrm{reg})}_t=
\left(\frac{\text{Close}^{SPX}_t-\text{Open}^{SPX}_t}{\text{Open}^{SPX}_t}\right)_{\text{shifted by }-1}.
\]
The binary target used for classification is:
\[
y^{(\mathrm{cls})}_t=\mathbb{1}\{y^{(\mathrm{reg})}_t\ge 0\}.
\]

\paragraph{(3) Lag features}
For lag set \(\mathcal{L}\), the model adds:
\[
\text{Close PC Lag }l:\ \text{Close PC}_{t-l},\qquad
\text{Open PC Lag }l:\ \text{Open PC}_{t-l},\quad l\in\mathcal{L}.
\]

\paragraph{(4) Rolling features with lookback \(k\)}
For each \(P_t\in\{\text{Close}_t,\text{Open}_t\}\):
\[
\mathrm{EMA}_t=\mathrm{EMA}_k(P)_t,\quad
\mathrm{VOL}_t=\mathrm{SD}_k(P)_t,\quad
\text{NormVol}_t=\frac{\mathrm{VOL}_t}{\mathrm{EMA}_t},
\]
\[
\text{ZScore}_t=\frac{P_t-\mathrm{EMA}_t}{\mathrm{VOL}_t}
\quad(\text{set to }0\text{ if denominator }=0).
\]
With channel bounds
\[
H^{\max}_t=\max_{j=t-k+1}^{t}\text{High}_j,\qquad
L^{\min}_t=\min_{j=t-k+1}^{t}\text{Low}_j,
\]
channel position is:
\[
\text{ChannelPos}_t=\frac{P_t-L^{\min}_t}{H^{\max}_t-L^{\min}_t}
\quad(\text{default }0.5\text{ when denominator }=0).
\]
Rolling VWAP uses typical price \(\text{TP}_t=(\text{High}_t+\text{Low}_t+\text{Close}_t)/3\):
\[
\text{RollingVWAP}_t=
\frac{\sum_{j=t-k+1}^{t}\text{TP}_j\cdot \text{Volume}_j}
{\sum_{j=t-k+1}^{t}\text{Volume}_j}.
\]

\paragraph{(5) Additional features and cleanup}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Day-of-week indicator and daily range \((\text{High}_t-\text{Low}_t)\).
  \item Forward-lag of open-based metrics (shift by \(-1\)).
  \item Raw non-stationary price columns (Close/Open/High/Low) are dropped after engineering.
  \item Rows with missing values are removed after joining predictors and target.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{(7) EDA sector--market correlation computation}
In EDA, sector daily return series are first built from ticker-level \(\text{Close PC}\) and then aligned by date with the S\&P 500 target return series \(y\). For sector \(s\), the equal-weight sector return is:
\[
R^{(\text{sector},\,eq)}_{t,s}=\frac{1}{N_{t,s}}\sum_{i\in s}\text{Close PC}_{t,i},
\]
where \(N_{t,s}\) is the number of available tickers in sector \(s\) at day \(t\). The market-cap weighted variant is:
\[
R^{(\text{sector},\,mcap)}_{t,s}=\frac{\sum_{i\in s}w_{t,i}\,\text{Close PC}_{t,i}}
{\sum_{i\in s}w_{t,i}},\qquad w_{t,i}=\text{MarketCap}_{t,i}.
\]
For each sector, correlation with the S\&P 500 series is computed using Pearson correlation:
\[
\rho_s=\mathrm{corr}\!\left(R_{t,s},\,y_t\right).
\]
