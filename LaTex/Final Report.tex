\section{Introduction}

\quad Predicting the stock market is challenging yet crucial for investors, traders, and researchers \cite{LIN_2024}. Rather than aiming to predict exact future stock prices, this study focuses on the more practical goal of forecasting the next-day direction of the U.S. stock market using historical data on prices and trading volume. The motivation for studying this problem lies in its potential implications for the financial industry as well as economic discussion. 

The predictive performance of our models may inform the investors and policy makers about potential market reaction to previous trends. In addition, it also provide evidence on supporting or contradicting the Efficient Market Hypothesis. If our models achieve meaningful out-of-sample predictive accuracy using only such historical data, this would suggest that these variables contain some predictive content and would therefore be inconsistent with weak-form market efficiency. Conversely, if the models perform poorly, the results would be more consistent with weak-form market efficiency.

\subsection{Research problem}

\quad \ Our project looks to forecast the direction of the S\&P500 Index of the following day (on a day-to-day basis). Consequently, we arrive at some potential conclusions regarding the question of how efficient the market is, and whether there are any meaningful patterns in the data that can be utilized to predict the market direction.

\subsection{Background context}

\quad According to \citeauthor{wolfers_2004}, the Efficient Market Hypothesis states that market prices fully reflect available information \endnote{The Efficient Market Hypothesis states that market prices fully reflect available information. The form of the hypothesis depends on the type of information incorporated into prices.In its strong form, prices reflect all information; in its semi-strong form, prices reflect all publicly available information; and in its weak form, prices reflect information contained in past prices and trading data.}. This implies that excess returns should not be systematically predictable from the relevant information set. Under the weak form of the Efficient Market Hypothesis\endnote{See the preceding endnote.}, historical price and trading-volume data should therefore not contain exploitable patterns for predicting future returns. If our models achieve meaningful out-of-sample predictive accuracy using only such historical data, this would suggest that these variables contain some predictive content and would therefore be inconsistent with weak-form market efficiency. Conversely, if the models perform poorly, the results would be more consistent with weak-form market efficiency.

The S\&P 500 Index is a stock market index that measures the stock performance of the top 500 companies in terms of market capitalization listed on the stock exchanges in the United States \cite{spglobal_2024}. This index is one of the most commonly followed equity indices and is often used as a benchmark for the overall performance of the U.S. stock market. The index includes companies from various sectors, including technology, healthcare, financial services, consumer discretionary, and more. The S\&P 500 Index is widely regarded as a leading indicator of the health of the U.S. economy and is closely watched by investors, analysts, and policymakers \cite{investopedia_2024}.

The computed valued of the index employs the float-adjusted market capitalization (FMC), which only takes into account the shares that are actually publicly traded on stock exchanges. This means that the companies with larger market caps are weighted more heavily, making the movements in their share price much more impactful on the broader index.

Stock market forecasting is a complex task that has been studied extensively in the field of finance and economics. However, because the stock market is influenced by a wide range of factors beyond company performance, including economic indicators, geopolitical events, and investor sentiment, accurately predicting the direction of the stock market is challenging. No single method is known to remain uniformly superior over time; rather, many newer approaches have offered modest improvements over earlier methods that were once widely used \cite{KUMBURE_2022}. This project will explore various recent statistical learning techniques to attempt to forecast the direction of the S\&P 500 Index, and evaluate their performance based on historical data.

One motivation for using only historical data is that market prices may already incorporate responses to broader economic and political conditions. Moreover, the contemporaneous economic and political indicators that influence the market are often not available for the forecast period. Futhermore, the use of only historical data allows us to evaluate the predictive content of this information set, which is relevant for assessing the weak-form efficiency of the market as mentioned above.

\subsection{Dataset description}


\quad \ The raw data was retrieved via Yahoo Finance's API \verb|yfinance|, containing the open price (OP), close price (CP), high price (HP), low price (LP), and traded volume (TV) for each stock. They will be mentioned as OHLCV features. The data utilized contain information on stocks that are and/or were listed on the S\&P 500 index during the time period spanning January 1st, 2018, to December 31st, 2025. The cleaning process pertained to evaluating the data for change in tickers\endnote{this usually happens when one or more company merges with another company}, invalid data \endnote{for example, when a stock was in a frozen state}, holidays where the market was closed, and missing data due to being listed or delisted during the time frame. All the observations with missing information of any of the 5 price metrics or trading volume were removed.

The raw data contains 2010 trading days and 2520 features. The sizable number of columns is explained by each stock (2010) and index (1) having their own price metrics for CP, OP, HP, LP and TV $([503+1] * 5)=2520$. The number of rows is explained by the exclusion of 19 holidays and 211 weekend days.

The features are then modified to create additional engineered features that are commonly seen used in quantitative technical analysis and trading strategies. These features include lag values, day-to-day percent change, volatility, max/min channel position, rolling volume weighted average prices, rolling z-scores, daily range, and the day of the week. Details of formula for each variable generation is provided in the Subsection \ref{subsec:feature_engineering} of the Appendix.

\subsection{Overfitting/underfitting analysis}


\quad \ A general note on overfitting and other issues with select models. Given by the fact that the raw data inherently has more features than observations, overfitting was a big issue, especially in earlier implementations of the model. Overfitting results in a train misclassification rate of zero perpetually and no amount of generalization was possible towards validation sets and test splits. Furthermore, partly because of this overfitting, when certain models (most notably logistic regression) would search for an optimal hyperparameter, since the training misclassification rate would be impeccable, if the lower bound of the $C$-values were small enough, the model would degenerate to always guessing 1 or 0 depending on the imbalance in the splits. 

This issue was mostly alleviated by introducing further dimensionality reduction methods (most notably grouping by sector and collinearity reduction), which reduced the dimensionality of the feature space enough for there to be any sense of meaningful training on the training set.

\subsection{Real-world implications}
 The accuracy of prediction in the best model is around 54\% suggests that there may be some predictive signal in the data. However, it is not very strong to consistently outperform random guessing. \subsection{Limitations}

  This study has several limitations. In terms of data, we only used historical price and volume information and did not include sentiment analysis, macroeconomic indicators, or alternative data sources. In terms of modeling, we did not explore mixed-model approaches or more complex deep learning architectures. In terms of evaluation, we mainly relied on accuracy, F1-score, and ROC-AUC under walk-forward validation and cross-validation, while additional selection rules (such as the 1SE rule based on k-fold cross validation) could be considered in future work. Finally, both time and computational resource constraints limited the extent of hyperparameter tuning, model experimentation, and large-scale cross-validation.

\subsection{Possible methodological extensions}

Future methodological extensions include more extensive hyperparameter tuning and broader model experimentation, along with a more comprehensive evaluation framework that incorporates additional metrics and validation techniques. The analysis could also be strengthened by including political and economic indicators, sentiment analysis, and other alternative data sources to improve predictive power. In addition, future work should explore more sophisticated modeling approaches, including deeper neural network architectures and hybrid strategies that combine machine learning with classical economic time-series methods such as ARIMA and GARCH.

\subsection{Details of Enginearing Features Before Dimensionality Reduction}
\label{subsec:feature_engineering}

Engineered predictors and targets are generated as follows:

\paragraph{(1) Percent-change base variables}
For each stock and day \(t\), percent-change features are computed from raw prices:
\[
\text{Close PC}_t=\frac{\text{Close}_t-\text{Close}_{t-1}}{\text{Close}_{t-1}},\quad
\text{Open PC}_t=\frac{\text{Open}_t-\text{Open}_{t-1}}{\text{Open}_{t-1}},
\]
and similarly for High/Low.

\paragraph{(2) Target construction}
The regression target is the next-day S\&P 500 open-to-close return:
\[
y^{(\mathrm{reg})}_t=
\left(\frac{\text{Close}^{SPX}_t-\text{Open}^{SPX}_t}{\text{Open}^{SPX}_t}\right)_{\text{shifted by }-1}.
\]
The binary target used for classification is:
\[
y^{(\mathrm{cls})}_t=\mathbb{1}\{y^{(\mathrm{reg})}_t\ge 0\}.
\]

\paragraph{(3) Lag features}
For lag set \(\mathcal{L}\), the model adds:
\[
\text{Close PC Lag }l:\ \text{Close PC}_{t-l},\qquad
\text{Open PC Lag }l:\ \text{Open PC}_{t-l},\quad l\in\mathcal{L}.
\]

\paragraph{(4) Rolling features with lookback \(k\)}
For each \(P_t\in\{\text{Close}_t,\text{Open}_t\}\):
\[
\mathrm{EMA}_t=\mathrm{EMA}_k(P)_t,\quad
\mathrm{VOL}_t=\mathrm{SD}_k(P)_t,\quad
\text{NormVol}_t=\frac{\mathrm{VOL}_t}{\mathrm{EMA}_t},
\]
\[
\text{ZScore}_t=\frac{P_t-\mathrm{EMA}_t}{\mathrm{VOL}_t}
\quad(\text{set to }0\text{ if denominator }=0).
\]
With channel bounds
\[
H^{\max}_t=\max_{j=t-k+1}^{t}\text{High}_j,\qquad
L^{\min}_t=\min_{j=t-k+1}^{t}\text{Low}_j,
\]
channel position is:
\[
\text{ChannelPos}_t=\frac{P_t-L^{\min}_t}{H^{\max}_t-L^{\min}_t}
\quad(\text{default }0.5\text{ when denominator }=0).
\]
Rolling VWAP uses typical price \(\text{TP}_t=(\text{High}_t+\text{Low}_t+\text{Close}_t)/3\):
\[
\text{RollingVWAP}_t=
\frac{\sum_{j=t-k+1}^{t}\text{TP}_j\cdot \text{Volume}_j}
{\sum_{j=t-k+1}^{t}\text{Volume}_j}.
\]

\paragraph{(5) Additional features and cleanup}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Day-of-week indicator and daily range \((\text{High}_t-\text{Low}_t)\).
  \item Forward-lag of open-based metrics (shift by \(-1\)).
  \item Raw non-stationary price columns (Close/Open/High/Low) are dropped after engineering.
  \item Rows with missing values are removed after joining predictors and target.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{(6) Sector aggregation and correlation filtering}
When sector mode is enabled, ticker-level features are averaged within sector:
\[
X^{(\text{sector})}_{t,m,s}=\frac{1}{|S_s|}\sum_{i\in S_s}X_{t,m,i},
\]
where \(m\) is the metric and \(S_s\) is the set of tickers in sector \(s\). 

\paragraph{(7) EDA sector--market correlation computation}
In EDA, sector daily return series are first built from ticker-level \(\text{Close PC}\) and then aligned by date with the S\&P 500 target return series \(y\). For sector \(s\), the equal-weight sector return is:
\[
R^{(\text{sector},\,eq)}_{t,s}=\frac{1}{N_{t,s}}\sum_{i\in s}\text{Close PC}_{t,i},
\]
where \(N_{t,s}\) is the number of available tickers in sector \(s\) at day \(t\). The market-cap weighted variant is:
\[
R^{(\text{sector},\,mcap)}_{t,s}=\frac{\sum_{i\in s}w_{t,i}\,\text{Close PC}_{t,i}}
{\sum_{i\in s}w_{t,i}},\qquad w_{t,i}=\text{MarketCap}_{t,i}.
\]
For each sector, correlation with the S\&P 500 series is computed using Pearson correlation:
\[
\rho_s=\mathrm{corr}\!\left(R_{t,s},\,y_t\right).
\]
